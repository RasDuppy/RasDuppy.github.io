From Scientific Notation to pH

The exponent becomes the integer part of pH. The coefficient shifts the decimal. This is the key insight students miss.

The Big Idea: pH = −log[H⁺] — the exponent gives the digits before the decimal, and the coefficient determines the mantissa (digits after the decimal).

Conjugate Acid-Base Pairs

In every Brønsted-Lowry acid-base reaction, each acid and its conjugate base differ by one H⁺.

Choose a reaction
The H⁺ Transfer Rule

Conjugate acid = base + H⁺  |  Conjugate base = acid − H⁺

A strong acid has a weak conjugate base (and vice versa). Amphoteric species (like HCO₃⁻ and H₂O) can act as either — they appear in both columns depending on the reaction partner.

Strong acid → weak conjugate base
HCl → Cl⁻ (nearly inert)
H₂SO₄ → HSO₄⁻
HNO₃ → NO₃⁻
Weak acid → stronger conjugate base
CH₃COOH → CH₃COO⁻
H₂CO₃ → HCO₃⁻
NH₄⁺ → NH₃

Buffers & Titration

Buffers resist pH change. Titration curves reveal equivalence points and pKa values.

pH = pKₐ + log([A⁻]/[HA])

At ratio = 1:1, pH = pKa. Effective buffer range: pKa ± 1. Outside this range, buffering capacity drops sharply.

Kw and Temperature

Water's ion product Kw increases with temperature. At 37°C, pure water has pH < 7 — but it is still neutral, because [H⁺] = [OH⁻].

The key insight students miss: "Neutral" means [H⁺] = [OH⁻], not pH = 7. pH = 7 is neutral only at 25°C. At body temperature (37°C), neutral pH is about 6.81.

Why does Kw increase with temperature?

The autoionization of water is endothermic:

H₂O(l) ⇌ H⁺(aq) + OH⁻(aq)    ΔH > 0

By Le Chatelier's principle, adding heat shifts equilibrium right → more H⁺ and OH⁻ → larger Kw. Since [H⁺] = [OH⁻] still holds, the solution remains neutral — just with a lower pH value.

AP/IB exam tip: If asked "is pure water at 37°C acidic?", the answer is NO — it is neutral. The equal concentrations of H⁺ and OH⁻ define neutrality, not pH = 7.
